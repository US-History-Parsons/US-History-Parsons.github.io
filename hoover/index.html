Hoover's Response to the Great Depression



  • Political philosophy

    • Believed in “rugged individualism” — people should rely on themselves, not the federal government.
    • Favored limited government intervention and voluntary cooperation between businesses and communities.
    • Thought direct federal aid to individuals could weaken independence.

  • Supported policies and programs

    • Encouraged businesses to keep wages and employment stable (voluntary cooperation).
    • Public works projects (e.g., infrastructure spending to create jobs).
    • Created the Reconstruction Finance Corporation (RFC) to loan money to banks, railroads, and big businesses.
    • Signed the Smoot-Hawley Tariff Act, raising tariffs on imports.
    • Supported limited relief through local/state governments rather than direct federal aid.

  • Positive actions

    • Took some action, unlike earlier presidents during downturns.
    • RFC helped stabilize parts of the financial system.
    • Expanded federal involvement in the economy more than previous presidents, setting precedents later used by Franklin D. Roosevelt.

  • Negative actions

    • Too cautious and slow — actions didn’t match the scale of the crisis.
    • Opposed direct federal relief to individuals, leaving many struggling.
    • Smoot-Hawley Tariff worsened global trade, deepening the depression.
    • Poor public communication, making him seem uncaring.
    • Events like the Bonus Army removal damaged his reputation.

  • Validity of the Criticisms of Hoover

    • Somewhat over-criticized, but not entirely unfairly.

      • Too much criticism: He did take action and expanded government more than people often realize.
      • Fair criticism: His response was insufficient and misjudged the severity of the crisis
    • Overall: He’s remembered negatively largely because his policies didn’t stop the Depression, especially compared to Roosevelt’s more aggressive approach.
